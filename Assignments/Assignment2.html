How poor data preservation nearly destroyed a feature film

An interesting article about the production of Toy Story 2 by OvationData tells of how the team nearly lost it all due to poor data management. During production, they kept their assets on an open Unix-based server so the hundreds of people working on the project could access the files. Because one animator made the mistake to type the command 'rm-r -f *' in the root directory, files were starting to be deleted recursively from the root folder, destroying all of the project files. By the time they pulled the plug, 90% of the project was gone. To make matters worse, it was discovered the backups were completely unusable. If it were not for one other person who had a copy of everything out of necessity as they were working remotely, Toy Story 2 could have ended up as a completely different movie.

Password Security in 2024: A Deep Dive into Best Practices

What does it take to keep your accounts secure these days? This question gets answered by Novatech in their article about password security. The main take away of the article was this:

  • Unique: Each password should be different for every account to ensure security if one account is compromised.
  • Complex: Passwords should be at least 12 characters long, including letters, numbers, and symbols. Avoid easily guessable information.
  • Managed with a Password Manager: Password managers generate, store, and autofill complex passwords, eliminating the need to remember or reuse them.
  • Passphrases: Passphrases, or sequences of unrelated words, are easier to remember and harder to crack than passwords. They should be at least 16-20 characters long.
  • Regularly Updated: Passwords should be changed every 60 to 90 days to reduce the risk of them being exploited.
  • Protected with Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): MFA adds extra security by requiring two or more factors (e.g., password and fingerprint).
With these techniques, you can be assured that your password is not your weakest security link.
